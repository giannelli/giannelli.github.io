Solution3.7.1.1

First thing to note is we have a problem at \(x=-1\): \begin{equation*}\at{\left(\frac{x-1}{x+1}\right)}{x=-1} = \frac{-1 -1}{-1 + 1} = \frac{-2}{0}\end{equation*}

The sage calculations below calculate finite limits at \(x=-1\). The graph does not shoot of to \(\pm\infty\) at this value.

Next see how the gradient behaves:

\begin{align*} f(x)&=\tan ^{-1} \left(\frac{x-1}{x+1}\right)\\ \text{Let }u&=\left(\frac{x-1}{x+1}\right)\\ \frac{df}{dx}&=\frac{df}{du}\frac{du}{dx}\\ \frac{d}{du}\tan ^{ - 1} u&=\frac{1}{1 + u^2 }\\ \frac{du}{dx}&=\frac{2}{(x+1)^2} \text{(Using quotient rule)}\\ \therefore \frac{df}{dx}&=\frac{1}{1 + u^2 }.\frac{2}{(x+1)^2}\\ &=\frac{1}{1+{\left(\frac{x-1}{x+1}\right)}^2}.\frac{2}{(x+1)^2}\\ &=\frac{(x+1)^2}{(x+1)^2+(x-1)^2}.\frac{2}{(x+1)^2}\\ &=\frac{1}{x^2+1} \end{align*}

Strange! This function seems to have a continous and well defined gradient at \(x=-1\).
