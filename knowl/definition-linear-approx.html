Definition3.5.3Linear Approximation

Given a twice continuously differentiable function \(f\) of one real number variable, Taylor's theorem for the case \(n = 1 \) states that: \begin{equation*} f(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x - a) + R_2\ \end{equation*} where \(R_2\) is the remainder term. The linear approximation is obtained by dropping the remainder: \begin{equation} f(x) \approx f(a) + f'(a)(x - a)\text{.}\label{men-6}\tag{3.5.4}\end{equation}This is a good approximation for \(x\) when it is close enough to \(a\); since a curve, when closely observed, will begin to resemble a straight line. Therefore, the expression on the right-hand side is just the equation for the tangent line to the graph of \(f\) at \((a,f(a))\). For this reason, this process is also called the tangent line approximation.
